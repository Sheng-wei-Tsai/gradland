## Goal

A practical, structured guide to navigating the Australian IT job market — specifically for international students and new graduates who weren't taught this in university. The guide covers six interconnected topics that form a complete picture of how to launch and grow an IT career in Australia: what paths exist, how grad programs work, how to write a competitive resume, what the job market actually looks like, what's in an offer, and how to think about long-term career building.

The content is based on lived experience in the AU market, not generic "career advice" recycled from US job boards.

## Six Topics

### 1. IT Career Pathways
Not all CS grads become software engineers. Australia's IT market has deep demand across:

| Pathway | AU Demand | Entry Roles |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Software Engineering | Very High | Graduate Developer, Junior SWE |
| Data / Analytics | High | Data Analyst, Junior Data Engineer |
| DevOps / Cloud | High | Cloud Support, Junior DevOps |
| QA / Test | Medium | Test Analyst, Graduate QA |
| Business Analysis | Medium | Graduate BA, Systems Analyst |
| IT Consulting | High | Technology Analyst, Graduate Consultant |
| Cybersecurity | Growing | SOC Analyst, Security Consultant |

Key insight: The AU market is smaller than the US. Specialisation matters earlier here — a generalist "full-stack + DevOps + data" resume reads as noise, not range.

### 2. Graduate Program Overview
Graduate programs are the most structured entry point into Australian IT. They typically:
- Run 12–24 months with rotations across 2–4 teams
- Pay $65k–$85k AUD base (Big 4 consulting) to $90k–$110k (product companies)
- Open applications: March–May for Feb/July start; some open in August
- Require Australian citizenship or permanent residency for most Big 4/government programs

**AU companies with notable grad programs:**
- Atlassian, Canva, Seek (product)
- CBA, ANZ, NAB (banking IT)
- Deloitte, Accenture, KPMG (consulting)
- IBM, DXC, Datacom (integrators)
- ATO, Service NSW, DSS (government — needs PR/citizenship)

**How to stand out:**
- Apply before September — most programs fill early
- Write cover letters that reference specific teams/products (not generic "passionate about technology")
- ICPC, hackathons, and personal projects matter more than GPA for product companies
- Communication skills are heavily assessed at consulting firms — practice case interviews

### 3. How to Write a Good Resume (AU Format)
Australian resumes differ from US and UK formats in key ways:

**AU-specific rules:**
- 2 pages max for graduates (1 page preferred for internships)
- Include: LinkedIn URL, GitHub, portfolio link — not a photo
- No objective statement — lead with a 3-line technical summary
- List projects before work experience if you have limited work history
- Quantify impact: "reduced API response time by 40%" beats "improved performance"
- Include relevant coursework only if it's non-obvious (e.g., distributed systems, not intro to programming)

**ATS-friendly formatting:**
- Standard fonts (Arial, Calibri, not custom fonts)
- No tables or columns — parse as single column
- Skills section: list tools/languages, not "Microsoft Office"
- Avoid: "Team player", "Hard worker", "Fast learner" — replace with evidence

**Tailoring for AU:**
- Use the job description's exact keywords (ATS matching)
- If applying to a consultancy, add a line about client-facing experience or communication
- If applying to a bank, mention security awareness, compliance, or financial domain exposure if relevant

### 4. Australia Job Market Overview
The AU IT market in 2025–2026:

**Market size and shape:**
- Sydney and Melbourne account for ~70% of IT jobs
- ~80,000 tech workers in Sydney, ~60,000 in Melbourne
- Brisbane and Perth growing fast due to government investment and resources sector
- AU market is approximately 1/10th the size of the US market — fewer total roles, but less competition than US for international applicants

**In-demand skills (2025–2026):**
- Cloud (AWS > Azure > GCP in AU)
- React / Next.js / TypeScript (dominant frontend stack)
- Python (data, backend, scripting)
- Kubernetes / Docker / Terraform (DevOps)
- Cyber security (government and banking driving demand)

**Common misconceptions:**
- "Just apply on LinkedIn" — most AU roles fill via referral or recruiter; direct applications have low conversion
- "I need 3 years experience" — grad programs explicitly hire 0 experience; apply anyway
- SEEK is more relevant than LinkedIn for AU mid-tier roles
- Most large AU companies sponsor 482 visas; smaller companies rarely do

**Salary expectations (2026, base only):**
- Grad / Junior (0–2 yrs): $70k–$100k
- Mid-level (2–5 yrs): $100k–$140k
- Senior (5+ yrs): $140k–$200k+
- Note: AU salaries include superannuation (11% on top); always compare "base + super" not just base

### 5. Typical IT Job Offer Components
Many grads accept offers without understanding what they've agreed to. A complete AU IT offer includes:

| Component | What to check |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Base salary** | Is it inclusive or exclusive of super? Always ask |
| **Superannuation** | 11% (2024) employer contribution — confirm it's on top of base |
| **Bonus** | Discretionary vs guaranteed; what % of base; last year's payout |
| **Equity / RSUs** | Vesting schedule (4yr / 1yr cliff is standard); what happens if you leave early |
| **Leave** | 20 days annual leave is standard; some companies offer 25+ |
| **Flexible work** | WFH days per week; core hours; flexible start/finish |
| **Visa sponsorship** | 482 / GTE support if on a student visa; confirm in writing before accepting |
| **Probation period** | Usually 3–6 months; limited redundancy protection during probation |
| **Notice period** | 2–4 weeks standard; big companies may lock in 1 month |
| **L&D budget** | Annual training/certification budget ($1k–$5k at good companies) |

**Red flags in offers:**
- Salary quoted without clarifying super inclusion
- "Base + super" split below market rate
- No WFH flexibility mentioned at all
- Bonus "at manager discretion" with no documented history

### 6. How to Build Your Career Path
The difference between developers who earn $150k at 30 and those still at $85k is rarely technical skill — it's career management.

**The compounding early career strategy:**
1. **Year 1–2:** Learn deeply. Choose a company that will teach you fundamentals, not just ticket-resolution. Avoid body shops unless there is no other option.
2. **Year 2–3:** Demonstrate scope expansion. Take on something outside your job description. Lead a small project. Mentor an intern.
3. **Year 3–4:** First job switch. This is where the salary jump happens in AU. Staying at the same company past year 4 as a junior/mid usually results in being underpaid vs market rate.
4. **Year 4–6:** Develop a specialisation. T-shaped skills: broad enough to work across the stack, deep in one area (e.g., distributed systems, ML infra, security).
5. **Year 6+:** Position yourself as a technical leader or move into a staff/principal IC track. Start building in public (GitHub, blog, talks).

**AU-specific career advice:**
- The AU network is small — treat every colleague as a future reference or hiring manager
- Seek has a well-run engineering blog; Canva, Atlassian, and CBA Tech all publish eng content — contribute or engage with this content
- Government grants fund tech innovation in AU: if entrepreneurship interests you, look at ARC grants, the CSIRO ON accelerator, or the Tech Council of Australia
- Permanent residency pathways for skilled migrants: 189/190/491 visas require points; IT roles qualify under ANZSCO codes. Plan this from year 1 if applicable.

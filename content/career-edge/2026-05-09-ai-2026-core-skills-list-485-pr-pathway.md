If you are on a 485 visa with a tech degree and you've been wondering which roles actually convert to permanent residence in 2026, this is the post.

The Department of Home Affairs publishes the [Core Skills Occupation List](https://immi.homeaffairs.gov.au/visas/working-in-australia/skill-occupation-list) (CSOL), which controls which jobs can sponsor you for the 482, 186, and 190 visas. The 2026 update — quietly released in the first quarter — added several AI-engineering occupation codes that previously sat in lower-tier lists. The practical effect: AU employers can now sponsor an AI Engineer through Direct Entry to a 186 visa with 3 years of work experience, instead of the slower Temporary Residence Transition pathway.

For an international graduate on a 485, this changes the strategy.

## Which roles got added

The 2026 CSOL update covers (verify on the [official ANZSCO list](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/classifications/anzsco-australian-and-new-zealand-standard-classification-occupations) — codes change):

- **Software Engineer (ICT)** — already on the list, but the AI specialisation pathway is now formal
- **Data Scientist** — broader scope, includes ML / AI roles
- **Cyber Security Engineer** with AI threat-detection responsibilities
- **DevOps Engineer** with explicit AI infrastructure / MLOps responsibilities
- **Machine Learning Engineer** — newly added as a distinct ANZSCO code

If your job title or duties match any of these, you are on the strongest visa-sponsorship track currently available in AU tech.

## What "matching" means in practice

Department of Home Affairs caseworkers don't read your job title in isolation. They read the [ANZSCO duties list](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/classifications/anzsco-australian-and-new-zealand-standard-classification-occupations) and compare to your *actual* day-to-day responsibilities, evidenced through:

- Job description provided by the sponsoring employer
- Reference letters from line managers
- Pay slips matching the threshold (currently AUD 73,150 for the Skilled income threshold, [TSMIT](https://immi.homeaffairs.gov.au/visas/employing-and-sponsoring-someone/sponsoring-workers/learn-about-being-a-business-sponsor/temporary-skilled-migration-income-threshold))
- Examples of work artefacts (the more public, the better)

The artefact part is where most 485 holders fail. Your job description says "Software Engineer", your actual work was "fixing minor bugs and writing CRUD endpoints", and there is no public artefact tying you to AI / ML work. The caseworker reads this and downgrades you to Software Engineer (ICT) — still sponsorable, but the pathway is slower and the income threshold higher.

## How to position for the AI codes — concrete moves

**1. Get the AI tasks added to your job description in writing.** Within your first 6 months at the sponsor, ask your manager to list AI/ML responsibilities explicitly: "Build and maintain LLM-powered features in production. Develop evaluation pipelines using LangSmith / Braintrust. Optimise prompt engineering for cost and latency." Even if these are 20% of your time, having them on paper matters.

**2. Build a public eval-driven artefact.** As covered in the [eval-driven side projects](./2026-05-08-eval-driven-side-projects-langsmith-braintrust-phoenix) post, a public GitHub repo with measured AI feature performance is the single highest-leverage portfolio piece in 2026. It evidences the AI specialisation in a way the caseworker can verify.

**3. Get one production AI feature shipped, even small.** A semantic-search bar in an internal admin tool counts. A LLM-powered email classifier counts. A RAG-based knowledge-base assistant counts. The artefact is the deployment to production, not the size of the feature.

**4. Reference letters that mention AI by name.** When you eventually leave the company (or after 12 months for the 186 application), the reference letter from your manager should say "Henry built the LLM-powered customer-support automation that reduced ticket volume by 40%" — not "Henry is a hard-working software engineer". Specificity is everything.

## The realistic 485 → 186 timeline in 2026

Based on r/cscareerquestionsAUS and AusITPros data over the last 12 months, international graduates landing AI engineering roles report:

- **Months 0–3 of 485**: job search, ~50–100 applications, 3–5 offers
- **Months 3–6**: first AI ticket shipped to production
- **Months 6–9**: full AI/ML responsibilities documented in role
- **Months 9–12**: nominated employer prepares 482 → 186 paperwork
- **Months 14–18**: 186 visa granted (Direct Entry stream)

Compare to a generic Software Engineer role (no AI specialisation): typical 24–36 months for 186 grant.

The gap between these two trajectories is what this category exists to help close.

## The 190 alternative — state nomination

If your sponsor isn't ready to nominate, the 190 (state nominated) visa is the backup. Each state publishes its own priority list. As of 2026:

- **NSW**: AI Engineer + Data Scientist on the priority list, fast-tracked
- **VIC**: ML Engineer + Cyber Security Engineer on the priority list
- **QLD**: Software Engineer + DevOps with AI experience, slower processing
- **SA**: aggressive points-based system, AI roles get bonus points

A 190 application requires the state to nominate you, which means submitting an Expression of Interest with your skills assessment. Get your skills assessed by [ACS](https://www.acs.org.au/cpd-education/migration-skills-assessment) — they evaluate AI roles using the same ANZSCO framework above.
